On the western edge of Gujarat, where the Arabian Sea meets a rocky coastline, stands one of Hinduism's most sacred sites. The Somnath temple. It has been demolished and rebuilt so many times that historians have lost count. Some say six times. Some say seventeen. The truth, as with most things involving Somnath, is complicated — and more extraordinary than any single number suggests.

## The First Temple

The earliest references to Somnath appear in the Skanda Purana, one of the eighteen major Puranas of Hindu tradition. The text describes a golden temple built by the moon god Soma himself — *Soma* meaning moon, *nath* meaning lord. Hence: the Lord of the Moon.

Historical records suggest a grand temple existed on this site by at least the 6th century CE, if not earlier. By the 10th century, it was one of the wealthiest temples in the world. Travellers described rivers of pilgrims, diamond-studded idols, and a treasury that made kings envious.

That wealth made it a target.

## The Raid of 1025

In January 1025, Mahmud of Ghazni rode into Gujarat with 30,000 cavalry.

What happened next has been recorded by Al-Biruni, by the Persian chronicler Al-Utbi, and by later Hindu texts — each with different details, but a shared brutal outcome. The temple was looted. The main idol was broken. Gold, jewels, and sacred objects were carried back to Afghanistan.

Al-Utbi, writing in the court of Mahmud, described the idol being broken in four pieces — two sent to Ghazni's great mosque in Afghanistan, one placed at the entrance of the royal palace to be walked upon, and one sent to Mecca.

The temple was rebuilt within twenty years.

## A Pattern Across Centuries

What followed was a pattern that would repeat across a thousand years:

Invasion. Demolition. Rebuilding.

Mahmud's raid was followed by the raids of Alauddin Khalji's general in 1299, by Muzaffar Shah in 1395, by Mahmud Begada in 1451, and — perhaps most completely — by Aurangzeb in 1702, who demolished the rebuilt temple and constructed a mosque on the plinth.

Each time, the temple was rebuilt — sometimes immediately, sometimes after decades. Sometimes the rebuilding was done by kings. Sometimes by merchants. Sometimes by devout individuals who pooled resources across generations.

The rebuilding was never simply architectural. It was a statement.

## The Last Reconstruction

After Independence, the restoration of Somnath became one of the first symbolic acts of the new Indian Republic.

Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, in one of his last significant acts before his death in 1950, took on the project personally. He visited the site, addressed a gathering, and oversaw the beginning of reconstruction. K. M. Munshi, writer and politician, became the driving force.

President Rajendra Prasad, not Prime Minister Nehru — who was privately skeptical of the project — consecrated the rebuilt temple on December 11, 1951.

The new temple, designed in the Chaulukya style of ancient Gujarati architecture, rose on the original plinth beside the sea.

## What Somnath Tells Us

There are temples older than Somnath. There are temples wealthier, grander, more architecturally complex.

But Somnath carries something different — a thousand-year argument between those who wanted to erase it and those who refused to let it be erased. It was not preserved by geography or obscurity. It survived, if survival is the right word, through repeated destruction and repeated will.

The temple you can visit today in Prabhas Patan, Saurashtra, is not the original. It is not the second, or the eighth, or the twelfth. It is the latest version of something that has been broken and remade so many times that the act of remaking has become inseparable from what the temple *is*.

Stand at the edge of the sea wall, where a pillar marks the spot and states that there is no land between this point and the South Pole — only open ocean — and you begin to understand why this place refused to disappear.

Some things outlast the forces that try to end them. Somnath is one of them.
